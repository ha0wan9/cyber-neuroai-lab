# Biological Credit Assignment — Learning-Rule Identifiability

Date: 2026-07-13  
Source: [Neuromatch Microlearning project notebook](https://instructor.neuroai.neuromatch.io/projects/project-notebooks/Microlearning.html)  
Status: structured draft; project direction not selected

## 1. Core Question

What measurements collected while a neural network learns are sufficient to
distinguish its update rule, and do those signatures generalize beyond the
architectures, hyperparameters, initializations, and measurement conditions
used to discover them?

This is narrower than asking which rule the brain actually uses. The proposed
work is a **virtual identifiability experiment**: candidate rules are known in
artificial networks, their observable consequences are measured, and a decoder
is tested on whether it can recover the rule label.

## 2. One-Sentence Formulation

Train matched networks with several candidate learning rules, summarize their
learning trajectories using experimentally motivated observables, and test
whether a classifier can identify the rule under held-out nuisance conditions
and degraded measurements.

## 3. What the Project Template Is Doing

The ten prompts form two branches after a shared setup:

1. **System construction (Q1–Q2):** build a feedforward classifier and replace
   backpropagation with candidate local or approximate rules.
2. **Signature extraction (Q3, Q7):** measure task performance, robustness,
   update signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), and cosine alignment with backpropagation.
3. **Descriptive comparison (Q4):** ask how performance differs across rules.
4. **Identifiability branch (Q5, Q8, Q9):** determine whether metrics predict
   the rule across seeds, hyperparameters, sparse sampling, and noisy or
   partial observations.
5. **Capability branch (Q6, Q10):** compare rules under online,
   non-stationary, or more complex learning settings.

The first four stages make a coherent project. The fifth is a different
research objective and is better treated as an optional extension.

## 4. Scientific Formulation

Let a training run be generated by

\[
\tau = F(r, a, h, s, d, t),
\]

where \(r\) is the learning rule, \(a\) the architecture, \(h\) the
hyperparameters, \(s\) the initialization/random seed, \(d\) the data
curriculum, and \(t\) the task or objective. A measurement operator
\(M_{q}\), parameterized by measurement quality \(q\), converts the full
trajectory into observable features:

\[
x = M_q(\tau).
\]

The inferential target is not merely high accuracy for a decoder
\(\hat r=g(x)\). It is high accuracy on runs whose nuisance variables were
not available during decoder fitting:

\[
P\!\left(\hat r=r\mid a,h,s,d,t\ \text{held out or shifted}\right).
\]

This framing exposes the main scientific issue: a decoder can succeed by
learning a rule-specific signature, or by exploiting a shortcut such as one
rule's systematically worse task accuracy, different update scale, or a
hyperparameter setting tied to the label.

## 5. Recommended Three-Question Project

### RQ1 — Within-regime identifiability (required)

Can learning rules be decoded from weight-update and activation-trajectory
features when architecture, initialization, task, training budget, and
performance are matched as closely as possible?

### RQ2 — Nuisance generalization (required)

Does decoding remain above chance when complete seeds and at least one
hyperparameter or architecture setting are held out?

### RQ3 — Measurement realism (optional if time permits)

Which observable family degrades most gracefully when checkpoints are sparse,
units are subsampled, and measurement noise is added?

The separate question “which rule learns best online or under distribution
shift?” should be a follow-up project unless the identifiability analysis is
finished early.

## 6. Variables and Controls

| Role | Variables |
|---|---|
| Target variable | learning-rule identity |
| Candidate rules | backpropagation plus 2–3 alternatives chosen for comparable task suitability; possible families include feedback alignment, Kolen–Pollack/sign-concordant feedback, and a perturbation rule |
| Observables | activation statistics across checkpoints; update norms and distributions; weight statistics; layer-wise activity changes; task loss/accuracy as an audited potential shortcut |
| Nuisance variables | initialization, learning rate, rule-specific tuning, hidden width, activation function, task difficulty, checkpoint timing |
| Measurement-quality variables | checkpoint interval, proportion of observed units/weights, additive noise |
| Unit of generalization | an independently trained network run, not an individual checkpoint from a run |

### Required controls

- Use paired initial weights wherever rule implementations permit.
- Give every rule a documented, bounded tuning budget; equal numerical
  learning rates are not necessarily an equal comparison.
- Split train/test data for the rule decoder by entire runs. Never place
  checkpoints from one run in both partitions.
- Hold out complete seeds and, for RQ2, complete nuisance settings.
- report a majority-class baseline and label-permutation control;
- test decoding both with and without loss/accuracy features;
- compare rule-balanced cells and report uncertainty across runs;
- check whether rule decoding persists within a matched performance band.

## 7. Hypotheses and Falsification

### Working hypotheses

- **H1 — working hypothesis:** learning rules will be distinguishable
  in-distribution from a combination of trajectory statistics.
- **H2 — source-informed expectation:** activation statistics will be more
  robust than weight statistics under severe unit subsampling and measurement
  noise.
- **H3 — working hypothesis:** decoding performance will fall under held-out
  architecture or hyperparameter settings, revealing that some apparent
  signatures are regime-specific.

### Falsification conditions

Treat a rule-specific signature as unsupported if decoding falls to chance
when complete runs are held out, disappears after matching task performance,
or fails under every held-out nuisance setting. Do not rescue the claim by
selecting a favorable feature set after inspecting the test conditions.

## 8. How to Interpret the Proposed Metrics

### Performance metrics

Accuracy, loss, learning speed, and input-noise robustness measure capability.
They can distinguish rules, but they are weak evidence about mechanism because
many implementation choices can change them.

### Update SNR

Update SNR is a scale-normalized description of consistency across examples.
It is better called an **update-statistics feature** than a direct estimate of
biological variance. Its meaning depends on what is averaged, over which
examples, and at what point in training.

### Cosine similarity to backpropagation

Cosine alignment measures whether a candidate update points in the same
parameter-space direction as the backpropagation update at the same state. It
is useful for studying optimization geometry, but it makes backpropagation the
reference—not the brain's unknown ground-truth rule. High alignment therefore
means “backprop-like,” not “biologically correct.”

### Rule-decoder accuracy

Decoder accuracy is the most direct measure of identifiability, provided the
split tests the intended generalization. A random checkpoint split mainly
tests memorization of run identity; held-out runs test new initializations;
held-out architectures or curricula test a stronger claim.

## 9. Main Conceptual Risks

1. **“Biological plausibility” is underspecified.** A method may solve weight
   transport while still requiring implausible error signals, update timing,
   or global coordination. Record plausibility constraints separately.
2. **Simple Hebbian learning is not a fair supervised baseline.** Poor MNIST
   accuracy can reflect objective mismatch rather than a general failure of
   local learning.
3. **Separability is not causal identification.** Success among a closed set of
   simulated rules does not show that any candidate is implemented by a brain.
4. **Performance can leak the label.** A decoder may identify the weakest rule
   from its loss rather than from a mechanistic signature.
5. **Checkpoint leakage inflates evidence.** Temporally adjacent checkpoints
   from one run are highly dependent.
6. **Aggregate features trade specificity for accessibility.** They may be
   experimentally measurable yet compatible with several distinct mechanisms.
7. **Artificial-network observables are only proxies.** Weight access, exact
   updates, and labels are much easier to obtain in simulation than in vivo.

## 11. Minimal Two-Week Feasibility Boundary

- Use one small task and one fixed base architecture for the required result.
- Compare three rules if implementations are already available; otherwise use
  two well-implemented rules rather than one correct and one fragile rule.
- Run enough independent seeds to estimate between-run variability; determine
  the final count after a timing smoke test rather than promising a large grid.
- Predeclare one primary observable family and one decoder.
- Make held-out seeds the minimum acceptable evaluation; add held-out width or
  sparse/noisy observation only after the core pipeline works.
- Stop with a credible negative result if decoding does not survive leakage
  controls or performance matching.

## 12. Evidence Status

### Established or directly source-supported

- The Neuromatch project explicitly frames credit assignment as the motivating
  problem and proposes comparing alternative rules with backpropagation.
- Its identifiability branch proposes clustering or classifying learning rules
  from metrics, then varying hyperparameters and measurement quality.
- Nayebi et al. trained classifiers on aggregate learning-trajectory
  observables and reported generalization tests involving held-out
  architectures/curricula, trajectory subsampling, unit subsampling, and added
  noise.
- In that study's tested settings, activation-derived statistics were more
  robust to unit undersampling and measurement noise than weight-derived
  statistics.

### Interpretation

- The strongest formulation is learning-rule identifiability under nuisance
  variation, not a leaderboard of biological plausibility.
- Q6 and Q10 create a capability branch that should be separated from the core
  identifiability claim in a short project.

### Open questions

1. Which two or three rule implementations are reliable enough for the course
   deadline?
2. Which observables correspond to measurements that could plausibly be made
   in the intended biological comparison?
3. How much rule decoding remains after excluding task-performance features?
4. Does a rule signature survive a genuinely unseen architecture or task?
5. How should recurrent e-prop/BPTT trajectory features differ from the
   feedforward features used in the template?
